About This Project

Why Space
Matters

This project explores why astronomy matters, how space technology works, and why the universe continues to inspire research and discovery.

Project Overview

Space science helps us understand how planets form, how stars evolve, and how Earth fits into a much larger universe. It combines physics, chemistry, engineering, and imagination.

Studying space also improves life on Earth through new technologies, satellite systems, medical tools, weather forecasting, and communication networks.

Did You Know?

8 min

Sunlight takes about eight minutes to reach Earth.

95

Jupiter currently has at least 95 known moons.

1 day

Venus rotates so slowly that one day lasts longer than one year there.

Infinite?

The whole universe may be far larger than the part we can observe.

Space Technology

How Humans
Explore Space

Rockets, telescopes, rovers, satellites, and stations all work together to help us learn about the cosmos.

Rockets

Rockets create enough thrust to escape Earth's gravity and carry astronauts, satellites, and probes into space.

  • Launch people and cargo
  • Deploy satellites and telescopes
  • Send missions toward the Moon, Mars, and beyond

Rovers and Probes

Robotic explorers collect data from places too dangerous or too distant for humans to reach directly.

  • Study Martian rocks and climate
  • Investigate asteroids and outer planets
  • Search for signs of past habitability

Satellites and Stations

Satellites orbit Earth for communication, navigation, and weather. Space stations allow long-term scientific research in orbit.

  • Track storms and climate
  • Enable GPS and global communication
  • Support experiments in microgravity

Solar System

More Worlds,
More Variety

This section now includes more planets, a filter, hover facts, exoplanets, and a comparison table to make the project feel more complete.

Mercury
Rocky planet

The closest planet to the Sun, with huge temperature changes between day and night.

Hover fact: A year on Mercury is only 88 Earth days long.
Venus
Rocky planet

Venus is wrapped in thick clouds and has a runaway greenhouse effect that makes it extremely hot.

Hover fact: Venus rotates so slowly that one day lasts longer than one Venus year.
Earth
Rocky planet

Earth is the only known planet to support life, with liquid oceans and a protective atmosphere.

Hover fact: About 71% of Earth's surface is covered by water.
Mars
Rocky planet

Mars is cold, dusty, and home to giant volcanoes, dried riverbeds, and evidence of ancient water.

Hover fact: Olympus Mons on Mars is the tallest known volcano in the solar system.
Jupiter
Gas giant

The largest planet, with powerful storms and dozens of moons, including Europa and Ganymede.

Hover fact: Jupiter's Great Red Spot is a storm larger than Earth.
Saturn
Gas giant

Saturn is famous for its bright rings made of ice and rock particles.

Hover fact: Saturn is so low in density it would float in a giant enough ocean.
Uranus
Ice giant

Uranus rotates on its side, giving it unusual seasons and a distinct bluish color.

Hover fact: Uranus has one of the coldest atmospheres in the solar system.
Neptune
Ice giant

Neptune is dark blue, windy, and very far from the Sun.

Hover fact: Neptune's winds can reach over 2,000 km/h.
Pluto
Dwarf planet

Pluto is a small icy world in the Kuiper Belt and remains one of the most famous dwarf planets.

Hover fact: Pluto has a heart-shaped glacier called Tombaugh Regio.

Exoplanets

Worlds Beyond
Our Solar System

Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars beyond our Sun. Learning about them makes the site feel broader and more advanced.

Why Exoplanets Matter

Exoplanets show that planetary systems are common across the galaxy. Some are giant hot worlds close to their stars, while others may be rocky and possibly more Earth-like.

  • Scientists study atmospheres for signs of gases and weather
  • Some exoplanets orbit in habitable zones
  • They help us compare our solar system to others

Black Holes and Galaxies

Beyond The Solar System

This section makes the website feel bigger than a planets project by exploring the structures and mysteries of deep space.

Black Holes

Black holes are regions of space where gravity is so strong that not even light can escape once it passes the event horizon.

  • Can form when very massive stars collapse
  • Some sit at the centers of galaxies
  • They can create jets and heat surrounding matter

Galaxies

Galaxies are giant collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter. The Milky Way is the galaxy that contains our solar system.

  • Spiral galaxies have rotating arms
  • Elliptical galaxies are more rounded
  • Galaxy collisions can reshape both systems

Mission Archive

Expandable Mission Cards

These cards can be expanded for more detail, and they can also be filtered by mission type or searched by keyword.

Apollo 11Moon - 1969

The mission that first landed humans on the Moon and became one of the most famous scientific achievements in history.

  • Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin walked on the Moon
  • Collected rock samples and scientific data
  • Proved crewed lunar missions were possible
Curiosity RoverMars - 2012

Curiosity investigates Mars to understand whether the planet once had conditions that could support life.

  • Found evidence of ancient water
  • Studies Martian rocks and atmosphere
  • Still supports long-term research
James Webb Space TelescopeTelescope - 2021

Webb observes deep space in infrared light to reveal distant galaxies, star birth regions, and exoplanet atmospheres.

  • Captured deep field galaxy images
  • Studies the early universe
  • Helps analyze atmospheric chemistry on distant worlds
